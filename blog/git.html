Rocky Start, Back on Track

Getting Instructions, Navigating Canvas, and Repository Messes

September 14, 2015

Phase 0 was a bit of a rocky start for me, but I'm happy to say, I'm almost all caught up and will be by the end of tonight. The anxiety I had building up to my DBC start day turned me into a bit of an insomniac. I'd go to bed around 11 and then wake up at 2 or 3 in the morning, unable to fall back asleep. The next day, I'd end up drinking upwards of 5-6 cups of coffee a day to try to stay awake at work. On the eve of Phase-0, Week 1, I'd found out that there were several more things I had to do before beginning, which were not listed in the prerequisites, so needless to say, that added to my panic. Luckily for me, it wasn't that big of a deal and some other people had issues as well. I was assured there'd be plenty of time to catch up.

Once I actually began doing the work, my anxiety pretty much went away all together. No more time to overthink and entertain myself with self-doubts, only time to work. I started out slow and steady, working about 2-3 hours a night, with the expectation that there'd be plenty of time for me to really power-through on the weekends, which is when I work better anyway, because I function better in the morning and usually coast off the momentum for the remainder of the day. On Thursday, however, came down with the flu, and that was my first valuable DBC lesson. Aside from, "don't get sick," which is often beyond our control, I would say, "don't put off your work." Because you might get sick, or, even more likely, you might not understand something well and need the help of your cohorts, which is difficult to get when you ask the Sunday night before the assignment is due.

All that being said, this week provided me with very good practice using the command line and Github repositories. At first I felt like I understood everything really quickly, which lead me to play around and experiement quite a bit, and that's where I got into trouble. I kept editing the files in my repositories and pushing them back out to git, but the problem was, I'd either keep making a new branch every time, or give one a name that already existed, and every time I went to make a change, I would re-clone the repository from Github, which would make my terminal really made at me. Got lots of errors, branches coming up in the color red instead of green, and even when I did manage to push a directory to Github, it would appear greyed out and inaccessible. What I finally did to remedy the problem, was extract all my files to my desktop, make new folders, and re-clone the Github repositories to my new, and better organized directories. In doing so, I learned that I didn't, in fact, need to re-clone a repository every time I needed to make a change to push to Github. It was a frustrating process, but I have to say, I think that in my struggle, I learned a lot about the nuiinces of Github and version control in general.

So that was my big learning experience for the week. Additionally, and this may be more valuable a lesson than anything mentioned above, was that I felt I was able to let go of a lot of intimidation I felt about programming. I remember months ago, I tried to teach myself Git and how to use the terminal and I failed miserably and thought that it was this big scary thing that I could never learn. Now here I am, just a week later, with a least a decent grasp on what they do, and a visualization of how they work. So I'm pretty pumped about that and also proud of myself for not throwing my hands up in the air and telling myself it's too hard to learn.
