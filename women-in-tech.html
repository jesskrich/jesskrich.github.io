Women in Tech: A Cultural Discussion

When I was given the option this week to research and write about a current cultural issue in tech, I felt compelled to talk about women in tech, or lack there of. Compelled, but also kind of bummed. Why bummed? Well for one, this is certainly not news. What kind of alternative perspective could I personally offer? Secondly, it sucks to look at the stats about women leaving the industry due to lack of senior level offers and hearing about how 'brogrammer' culture is too alienating for most of us to take. At a time when coding is so new and exciting to me, writing about this feels like a downer. Or at least it did at first, but the more I thought about it, the more optimistic I became.

I guess, initially I didn't put too much thought into how this came to be. I just sort of accepted the idea that maybe women just weren't as interested in this type of work as much men, but when I looked into it, I found that nearly 50 years ago, about half of all tech workers were female, and as recently as 1984, women made up approximately 40% of the workplace. The current current census, which hovers at about 25%, is a staggering drop, comparitively. So what happened?

According to this article, programming used to be seen more as women's work until household computers became common. These computers were marketed as boys' toys, I would imagine due to their hefty price tag and the fact that most women wouldn't have been able to comfortably afford them back then. This, very likely, inspired a whole generation of men in tech.

While this explains the ratio of men in tech being so high, it doesn't necessarily hint at why the hostility of brogrammer culture exists. There are plenty of other male-dominated industries, like finance, and while women who work in one of those will be the first to admit it often feels like a boys club, rarely is it so bad that they feel like it's cause to change career paths.

Here is where I go on my own tangental theory. Computers, while marketed toward men and boys, maybe did not particularly hold the attention of the popular guys for very long, and as time went on and boys grew up, the more outgoing, confident guys started spending more time at sports practices, parties, and dating girls. Meanwhile, the nerds stuck around at home on their computers. These were the social outcasts- the guys who never got the girl. Who probably felt, much as women in tech tend to, that their presence was unwanted. They likely developed a sense that their only asset was their intellect.

Flash forward to 10 years later when these guys are working as engineers and a woman walks onto their team. If she is a person who is socially well-adjusted and more of a people-person, I would imagine it would feel pretty threatening to have the one thing you thought you were good at suddenly become just another thing someone else is good at, and though you have to be civil for the sake of HR, resentment is bound to seep through.

Luckily for everyone, I do believe that big changes are coming. For one, the tech industry has recognized how badly women are needed and has taken action to encourage us to pursue this career path. Not only is it imperative that more women join the workforce in order to keep up with the demand, but according to Life Hacker, businesses with a woman executive on the team tend to have significantly higher valuations at first and last funding.

Secondly, I have seen a shift in cultural values in the last decade, which puts intellect on a higher pedestal than athleticism. I think this will serve to give male programmers a boost in confidence, consequently reducing workplace hostility. With billionare tech founder idols like Mark Zuckerberg and Sergey Brin, nerds are the new rockstars, and there's plenty of tension-free room for everyone in the community.
